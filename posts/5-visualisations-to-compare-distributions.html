Visualisation • Men and Women inequalities • Toucan Toco charts



Distributions can be visualized with a tones of charts. This very cool article draw an inspiring inventory of visualization. Now, I would like to try to compare two distributions. Without listing them all (the combinations of shape, colors, and computations are infinite right ?) I propose 5 charts, illustrating with two simple distributions: the average hourly wages of men and women inside a random sample of 25 French municipalities. I then got the a distribution of 25 men hourly wages (each of them is an average in any french town) and 25 women hourly wages (each of them is an average in any french town)

Source: I got the data from Insee here and prepare with this pandas script.

First of all, let's choose a nice color code two distinguish my two distributions. Men are in blue, and women in red. In the visualizations, you can choose to display both distributions or only men or women with the following selector.

Simple plot

Each datum are represented by a point. Very simple and quite straightforward. We get some overlap, I have tried to handle, without much success with color opacity. But this first preview, let us presume that men hourly wage tend to be higher than women. (Inside the 50 random sample).

To handle the overlap, it's a good trick to add a random vertical translation. Inside the 50 random sample, men wage tend to be higher than women's.

Sorted data

Very easy to understand, this is my favorite visualization. Human love sorting stuff. It creates order and harmony without mislead the data. Women are much more present on the right (small wages) and men are more present on the left (higher wages)

It's also possible to turn the bar in order highlighting the top or the bottom. Use the button to visualize either top or bottom.
I added the median of the total sample. Hum... much more blue bar above the median, and much more red below.



N to N Comparisons

Very efficient: comparing smallest value of each distribution, then second smallest value, and any n-th value of both distribution. Here we see that smallest men hourly wage is greater than the smallest women hourly wage. Same, for second smallest, and same for all.


We can replace bars by with lines, even if it breaks the But it make very clear that blue line (men wage) is above the red one (women wage).

Cumulative distribution function

How many observations below this hourly wage ? answer the cumulative distribution function answer. Looking at this chart, I can tell you that around 20 women and 10 men got an hourly wage below 14€
